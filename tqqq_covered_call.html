📐 The Core Mechanic — Call Price vs Share Price

When you sell a covered call, the call option price moves opposite to your interest as the seller:

TQQQ price goes UP → call becomes MORE valuable → bad for you as seller TQQQ price goes DOWN → call becomes LESS valuable → good for you as seller This is opposite to how you think about your shares: Shares go up = your wealth increases Call price goes up = your liability as seller increases

🔄 Why the 50% Rule Still Applies

You sell $50 call for $1.80. TQQQ stays flat. Week 1: call worth $1.60 (slow theta) Week 2: call worth $1.10 (theta accelerating) Week 3: call worth $0.90 → GTC fires. Close here. Why not wait for $0.05 (let expire)? Inside 21 DTE, a single Nasdaq news event can spike TQQQ 8–10% and turn a $0.90 call into a $4.00 call overnight. The last $0.90 of premium is NOT worth that risk. Close at 50%, sell a fresh call, reset the clock.
⚠️ TQQQ-Specific Gamma Warning
TQQQ's 3× leverage amplifies gamma effects vs SPY. A 2% Nasdaq move = ~6% TQQQ move. Inside 21 DTE, this can turn a comfortable OTM call into a deep ITM problem within hours. The 50% / 21 DTE rule is even more critical here than on SPY.

🎯 Strike Selection — The Happy-to-Sell Test

1
Would I be happy if my shares were called away at this price? If yes → proceed. If no → go higher. Never sell a call at a strike you'd regret.
2
Is the strike above my cost basis? Always. Selling a call below cost basis means locking in a loss if called away.
3
Delta check: Zone B → delta 0.20–0.25. Zone C → delta 0.25–0.30. Higher delta = more premium, higher chance of assignment.
4
OTM% sanity check: With VIX ~24, target at least 8–12% OTM for TQQQ. A 4% Nasdaq rally = 12% TQQQ move — your strike needs breathing room.

For Zone B and C — assignment is NOT a bad outcome. It IS the exit strategy. When your call goes ITM in these zones, that's your plan working. Don't panic-roll just to avoid losing shares you intended to sell anyway.

🛡️ Non-Negotiable Rules for TQQQ Covered Calls

1
Never sell calls on Zone A shares. Every covered call is a potential exit. Zone A (200 shares) always stays uncovered — this is your upside insurance.
2
Never sell a call below your cost basis. If TQQQ drops to $37 and you sell a $39 call for premium, you're locking in a loss on exit. Strike must always be above cost basis.
3
No calls the week before major macro events. Fed, CPI, big-tech earnings. TQQQ is 3× leveraged — a single event can blow your strike in hours.
4
21 DTE is the hard close trigger — no exceptions. Gamma on a leveraged ETF inside 21 DTE is brutal. A 3% Nasdaq drop can move your call 4× more than it would outside 21 DTE.
5
VIX above 35 → pause new calls. Market dislocation can cause TQQQ to gap 15–20% in a single session. Higher IV means fat premiums but also real blow-up risk.
6
Keep a trade log. Every call: zone, entry date, strike, expiry, premium, close date, outcome. Track which zone each trade belongs to — critical for managing the full 600-share picture.

⚡ The TQQQ Volatility Decay Reality

TQQQ has a structural problem: volatility drag. Over long periods, 3× leveraged ETFs underperform 3× the index return due to daily rebalancing math. Example: Day 1: QQQ -5% → TQQQ -15% (now at $85 from $100) Day 2: QQQ +5% → TQQQ +15% (now at $97.75) Net: QQQ flat, TQQQ down 2.25% — this compounds daily. This is why your covered call income is especially valuable here: You're monetising a position that structurally erodes over time. Collected $1.80/share × ~6 cycles over 6 months = ~$10.80/share On a $38 cost basis, that's a 28% effective cost basis reduction. Even if TQQQ stays flat, you're winning.
